Boost Business with Impactful Marketing Campaigns

Marketing is an essential skill for any business owner. Regardless of if you choose to work with a marketing team, hire a marketing manager or do it for yourself, understanding some timeless principles will help you build a better marketing strategy to take your salon or spa to the next level.

Marketing, put simply, is connecting with your ideal customers and letting them know that you’re there to help with their beauty and wellness needs. Say your specialty is getting the perfect icy blonde for your clients, it’s imperative you let the icy blonde hopefuls in your area know that you can help them get the look they’re after. They need you and having the right marketing strategy lets them know where to find you.

Just like having all the right tools for the perfect haircut, this course will fill your marketing toolbox with everything you need to flourish. Starting with the basics of marketing, you’ll discover how to engage with new and existing customers in an effective and meaningful way.

Marketing 101

If you’ve ever gone to a restaurant with a menu that’s way too long and ended up ordering the most basic dish you typically make at home, you’ve experienced decision overwhelm. The US hair care industry includes more than 80,000 salons and/or barbershops, so you can imagine how overwhelming it may feel for potential customers to find the right salon - to find YOU. Don’t be afraid to stand out from the crowd. Having a quality marketing strategy removes decision overwhelm and drives customers directly to you.

Let’s look at Kylie Cosmetics, for example.

Founded in 2015, the company is worth an estimated $900 million. There are a lot of brands that sell lipsticks and lip kits but none as popular as Kylie Cosmetics. Founded by Kylie Jenner, the company skyrocketed due to Kylie’s social influence, from a wildly popular TV show to a massive Instagram following, Kylie Cosmetics is a prime example of the value and importance of marketing. Essentially, by creating her own line of products, Kylie has taken decision overwhelm away from her followers. If you’re a fan of hers and lipstick, it’s an easy decision to make to pick up her newest lip kit.

Building Your Strategy

The foundation of your strategy must start with your target audience or ideal customer. You are committed to embracing a deeper sense of power in your business and are excited to learn how to find more ease and joy in the process. Start with understanding who you are talking to by getting to know your ideal customer.

Before you skim past this section or generalize who this person is, we invite you to get out your pen and paper and set aside some time to consider the following:

Who is your ideal customer?

  • What is their age?

  • What is their gender?

  • Where do they live?

  • Where do they shop?

  • What websites do they visit?

  • Where do they get their information (example: online, word of mouth, etc)?

  • What do they do for a career?

  • What is their socio-economic situation?

  • What goal are they hoping to achieve (example: 70's bangs are back and they want them STAT)?

  • What is a “pain point” for them (example: they work shifts and have obscure availability so it’s hard for them to get an appointment during traditional hours)?

Now that you know your ideal customer it is time to consider your brand.

What is your brand?

  • What does your salon or spa specialize in (hot tip: it can be one thing! You don’t have to be for everyone)?

  • What are your hours?

  • Where is your location(s)?

  • What is your brand model (premium, affordable, family-friendly, niche, etc.)?

  • What is the overall feeling you want to convey?

  • What is your salon aesthetic and how does that carry forward to your marketing?

  • What is the experience you want clients to have with your brand?

Next, it is time to look at your delivery method.

Are you going to use digital or print or a combination of both? There’s no right or wrong answer or a “one-size-fits-all” method. Let’s look at options so you can make an educated decision on what is best for your business.

Marketing to New Customers

Acquiring new customers comes with a price tag. It is anywhere from 5 to 25% more costly to acquire a new customer than it is to retain an existing one. And in fact, the cost of acquiring new customers has increased 60% in less than 10 years.

Yes, you want to attract new customers if you’re looking to expand your locations or increase your client base. So knowing the high cost involved, being as strategic as possible is key.

Now that you know your ideal customer, your brand ethos, and the medium(s) you wish to use, it’s time to consider your strategy for both new and existing customers.

Attracting new customers to your brand is called acquisition marketing and while the elements may be the same, the intention is different. You want to invite people in through brand awareness and encourage them to complete your desired call to action (CTA).

Consider targeted promotions for these potential customers. That can look like:

  • Introductory pricing
  • New guest or first-time service offers
  • Gift card promotions- after all, today’s gift is tomorrow’s guest
  • Sharing an enticing brief overview of service offerings
  • Reviews, reviews, reviews!
  • Location awareness

Let’s deep dive into 5 and 6 because these are often pushed to the bottom of the list (see above!), but they are crucially important.

Marketing to Current Customers

The original 80/20 rule has nothing to do with the ratio of veggies to carbs on your plate. It’s been shown that upwards of 80% of a company’s revenue comes from only 20% of its customers. Just like broccoli and green veggies are an important part of a balanced diet, retaining existing clients has significant impacts on the health of your business.

Current customers are important to your business because they:

  • Typically spend more, 67% more to be exact
  • Consider recommendations of new products or services
  • Give you a second chance if they experience any issues
  • Refer family and friends, referred clients have 16% higher lifetime value than a client who wasn’t referred
  • Provide valuable feedback that helps you strengthen your business
  • Review, review, review! These are the people you want to invite to leave reviews and recommend your services to friends wherever possible. You can do this by incentivizing them or simply asking.
